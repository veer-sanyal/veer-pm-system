# The 12-Week Initiation Protocol

*Companion to `Charisma_and_Social_Anxiety_Research_Report.md`. That document is the why; this is the what. Every element below maps to a verified mechanism in the report.*

**Timing note:** started mid-June 2026, this runs through early September. That is deliberate. Weeks 1-9 land in summer (lower social density, perfect for building the machine on recruiting chats, user interviews, and low-stakes reps). Weeks 10-12 land on the start of fall semester, which is the highest-leverage friend-formation window of the year. You will hit the best window of sophomore year already warmed up, instead of spending it warming up. You already know what it cost to be cold during freshman fall.

---

## The rules of the game (read once, then they're law)

### Rule 1: Everything is an experiment, and experiments get logged

Every exposure is three lines, written before and after. Keep it wherever you'll actually use it (notes app, this repo, anywhere):

```
PREDICTION: specific, falsifiable, with a probability.
  "If I text Arjun to lift tomorrow, 60% he leaves me on read or flakes."
TEST: what I did, and which safety behaviors I kept OFF.
RESULT: what literally happened. Not how it felt. What happened.
```

The log is the whole engine. By week 12 you will have 60+ entries, and the gap between your predicted rejection rate and your actual rejection rate is the dataset that rewires the hesitation. Anxiety is a miscalibrated model; you are collecting the training data to correct it. Skipping the log turns exposures back into vibes.

### Rule 2: Safety behaviors off, one at a time

Week 1 you'll write your personal top five. Common candidates given your pattern:

1. Drafting and re-drafting messages before sending (cap: one draft, send within 2 minutes of writing it)
2. Waiting for the "right moment" to join or speak (the right moment is a safety behavior wearing a strategy costume)
3. Phone-checking in groups when nobody's talking to you
4. Keeping questions shallow to avoid imposing
5. Pre-scripting your half of conversations

Each week's exposures name which banned behavior stays off. Doing the rep with the safety behavior on counts as half a rep, because the report's Part 1.2 is clear: the protection is what's maintaining the problem.

### Rule 3: The post-event debrief replaces the post-mortem

After any significant interaction you get exactly 3 minutes, written, same four questions every time:

1. What did I predict? What happened? (pull from the log)
2. One thing that objectively went fine.
3. One thing to adjust next rep (behavioral, not "be less awkward").
4. Did I act, given the anxiety? (That's the win condition. Not "was I anxious." You don't control that, and per the report you don't need to.)

Then closed. If your brain reopens the case at 1am, the rule is: "Already debriefed. Nothing new in the file." Rumination is your reflective engine pointed at corrupted footage; this gives it a scheduled, bounded outlet and starves the 1am session.

### Rule 4: If-then plans, because the gap is the trigger moment

Your failure mode is not refusal, it's deferral. So every week's actions get pre-committed as if-then plans during the Sunday review. Format: "When [concrete trigger], I will [concrete action] immediately." Examples seeded throughout below. The decision gets made on Sunday; weekdays are just execution.

### Rule 5: Minimums, not maximums

Weekly minimum: **3 logged exposures + 1 charisma rep + Sunday review (15 min).** A week where you do the minimum is a successful week. Miss a week entirely? Resume at the same phase, no makeup debt, no narrative about being behind. The habit research says missed days don't derail formation; the story you tell about missed days is what derails it.

### Rule 6: Difficulty ratchet

Rate every exposure 0-100 for anticipatory anxiety (SUDS). The sweet spot is 40-70: real resistance, doable anyway. If your reps are landing under 40 two weeks running, you're hiding inside the protocol. Escalate.

## Phase 1 (Weeks 1-2): Build the machine

Goal: infrastructure plus proof you'll run small reps. Nothing here is hard. That's the point; you're learning the loop, not testing courage.

**Week 1: Baseline.**
- Write your **fear ladder**: 15-20 specific initiation actions across your four arenas (Purdue friends, recruiting, hosting, user interviews), each with a SUDS rating, sorted. Bottom rungs ("ask a question in a group chat") to top rungs ("host a thing I organized," "cold-call style ask to someone senior").
- Write your **top 5 safety behaviors** (Rule 2).
- Log 3 trivial exposures to learn the format: ask a stranger a question (time, directions, recommendation), make eye contact and nod at someone in the gym, send one unedited text to someone you haven't talked to in a while. Prediction first, every time.
- **Charisma rep:** in one conversation, your only job is follow-up questions. Not impressive statements. Their last sentence is the prompt for your next question. Notice how it changes what your attention is doing (outward, not inward). That attention shift is simultaneously the anxiety fix and the charisma move; it's why this rep is first.

**Week 2: Calibration week.**
- 3 exposures from the bottom third of your ladder, one per arena minimum.
- One **reach-out rep**: message someone you've been meaning to contact and haven't. Verbatim-level template: "Hey, random but you crossed my mind. [One specific thing: saw X, remembered Y]. How's your summer going?" One draft, send. Per Liu et al. in the report, the staleness you're worried about is precisely what makes it land better.
- **Charisma rep:** record yourself for 2 minutes explaining your study platform idea (you'll reuse this in user interviews and recruiting). Listen once for pitch flatness and pause usage only. No general self-critique; that's rumination with a clipboard.
- Sunday: first full review. Tally predictions vs results. This is the first data the new self-concept gets built from.

## Phase 2 (Weeks 3-5): Volume at low stakes

Goal: make initiating *frequent* before making it *hard*. Friendship math (50/90/200 hours) means volume and repetition do most of the work, so we install volume structures now.

**Week 3: The reach-out engine.**
- 5 reach-outs this week: 2 dormant Purdue connections, 2 career-relevant (a SMIF upperclassman, an EPICS teammate from last year, an IBE senior), 1 wildcard. Same template discipline: one specific detail, one question, one draft, send.
- If-then: "When I think 'I should text this person,' I send it within 2 minutes or it counts as an avoided exposure and gets logged as one." Naming the avoidance in the log is the enforcement mechanism; you hate an honest bad metric more than you hate sending the text.
- **Charisma rep:** follow-up questions again, but now add one **self-disclosure**: offer something real about yourself unprompted (a current struggle with the product, what you're actually unsure about for recruiting). Depth is reciprocal and someone goes first. You're someone.

**Week 4: Coffee chat reps (recruiting arena).**
- Send 4 coffee-chat asks: Purdue alumni in PM/strategy/consulting via LinkedIn or the alumni directory. Script: "Hi [name], I'm a Purdue IBE sophomore interested in [their specific area, one sentence on why them specifically]. Would you have 20 minutes in the next couple weeks? Happy to work around your schedule." Prediction first: write down your expected response rate. (Flynn & Lake says you'll lowball it by roughly half. Test it.)
- Take every chat that lands. In the chat: follow-up questions, one real disclosure, and end with a direct ask: "Is there anyone you think I should talk to?" That sentence is itself an exposure rep.
- **Charisma rep:** in one chat, deliberately use contrast or a micro-story when answering "tell me about yourself," instead of resume recitation.

**Week 5: Propinquity infrastructure.**
- Pick or create **2 recurring weekly structures** for fall and commit now while it's cheap: a club with weekly meetings, an intramural team, a standing gym time with a specific person, a weekly study block in the same spot. The report's verdict is blunt: friend groups are scheduled, not found. Recurring structure means the 90 hours accumulate without a fresh act of courage each time.
- 3 exposures from the middle of your ladder.
- **User interview rep:** ask 2 people to talk about how they study for 15 minutes. Strangers or near-strangers preferred (variability principle). This is triple-counted on purpose: exposure, product research, and conversational reps with built-in purpose.

## Phase 3 (Weeks 6-8): Invitations, depth, hosting

Goal: move from contact to connection. This is where the inviter identity gets built, because it's the rep with the most evidence-value per attempt: an accepted invitation is undeniable data that people want to spend time with you.

**Week 6: Invite first.**
- 3 invitations to one-on-one or small-group time: "grabbing food Thursday, come" / "I'm hitting the gym at 6, join" / "want to get coffee and trade recruiting notes?" Specific time and activity in the first message; "we should hang sometime" is a safety behavior because it can't be refused and therefore can't be accepted.
- Prediction per invite. Liking-gap correction: the people you'd rate as "probably neutral on me" likely rate you higher than you think. Invite from that corrected estimate.
- **Charisma rep:** one conversation where you go one notch deeper than the established level with that person (fast-friends gradient). One sincere question you actually want the answer to: what they care about, what they're worried about for the year, what they'd do if recruiting didn't exist.

**Week 7: Depth week.**
- 2 one-on-ones with people from week 6 or existing friends. The experiment: drop the topic one full level ("honestly, my main fear about sophomore year is...") and log the response. Kardas et al. says depth lands better than predicted. Test it on your own data.
- 2 more coffee chats or follow-ups from week 4. Follow-up message to a previous chat: one line on what you did with their advice. This is the warmth signal almost nobody sends, which is exactly why it compounds.
- 2 more user interviews.

**Week 8: Host something.**
- One hosted event, deliberately small: 3-5 people, low production. Pizza and a movie, group dinner at a dining court, study-then-food session, pickup basketball you organized. The bar is "you initiated it, you invited them, it happened."
- Prediction first, in detail: how many say yes, how it goes. This is a top-half-of-ladder rep and the single most identity-loaded experiment in the protocol, because "I'm the kind of person who hosts" is unfalsifiable by anxiety once it has literally happened.
- Debrief by Rule 3 only. Three minutes, four questions, closed.

## Phase 4 (Weeks 9-10): Hard mode

Goal: attack the actual fear, which is not conversation. It's being seen to want something and getting rejected. So now you practice wanting things visibly, including where rejection is likely.

**Week 9: The asking week.**
- 5 direct asks where rejection is realistic: a senior person for a 20-minute call, a professor about research or a project, a discount you have no business asking for, someone impressive to look at your product, a slot to present at a club. Mix arenas.
- Target: **at least one real rejection.** If everything lands, your asks were too safe; escalate until something bounces. A rejection obtained on purpose, predicted in writing, and survived, is the rep that does the most work. That's Hofmann's mishap logic plus Jia Jiang's anecdote, run on your own data.
- The log entry for a rejection gets one extra line: "Cost of this no, observed, in reality:" and you write down what actually happened after. (Usually: nothing.)

**Week 10: Mishap exposures + fall semester opens.**
- 2 deliberate small mishap reps: ask an oddball question in public, be mildly and harmlessly awkward on purpose, take up space you'd normally cede (send the food back politely, ask the "dumb" question in a meeting first). The point is to collect direct evidence about what actually happens when you look slightly off. Spoiler from the literature: approximately nothing, which is the most anti-anxiety data there is.
- Fall semester is starting. Activate the week 5 recurring structures immediately, first week, while everything is socially liquid.
- 3 first-week initiations: introduce yourself to people in two classes (sit in the same seat, same neighbors, let propinquity grind for you), and one new-person invitation within 72 hours of meeting them. Fast follow-up while the contact is warm is the highest-conversion move in the entire plan.

---

## Phase 5 (Weeks 11-12): Blitz and consolidation

**Week 11: Max leverage week.**
- This is the easiest week of the year to make friends at Purdue and you are 10 weeks trained. Run volume: every recurring structure attended, 2 invitations, 1 reach-out, classes worked. Everyone is looking for their people right now; the inviter wins disproportionately in week one of fall.
- One hosted event, round two. Bigger or repeated, your call.

**Week 12: Read the data, design the permanent system.**
- Full log review, 30-45 minutes, treated like a product retro:
  - Predicted rejection/awkwardness rate vs actual, computed. (This number is the whole protocol's payload. Put it somewhere you'll see it.)
  - Which arena moved most. Which safety behavior is actually extinct. Which ladder rungs that were 60+ SUDS in June are now boring.
  - The honest one: where did you hide inside the protocol? (Reps done in the easiest arena only? Logs skipped after bad days?)
- Design the maintenance system, smaller but permanent: 2 initiations + 1 deeper rep per week, Sunday review stays, log stays for anything above 50 SUDS, debrief rule stays forever.
- Decide what the next 12 weeks' version looks like with fall recruiting and the product as the arenas.

## Failure modes, named in advance (because they're predictable)

1. **The protocol becomes the content.** You refine the ladder, re-read the report, optimize the log format, and call it progress. That is the partial-action pattern eating its own cure. The plan is finished; only reps count now. If you notice yourself editing this document instead of doing week N, that's an avoided exposure. Log it as one.
2. **All reps in the comfortable arena.** User interviews have built-in purpose, so they'll tempt you as the everything-rep. Variability is a verified principle; rotate all four arenas.
3. **Anxious rep counted as failed rep.** Re-read Rule 3, question 4. Acting while anxious is the entire mechanism. Calm is a lagging indicator and chasing it is how safety behaviors sneak back in.
4. **A genuinely bad outcome triggers protocol abandonment.** Some rep will actually go poorly; with 60+ reps, base rates guarantee it. One bad outcome in a log of dozens is a functioning dataset, not a verdict. Debrief it in 3 minutes like everything else.
5. **Quiet weeks compound into a quiet month.** Minimum week is 3 exposures + 1 rep + review. If you can't do the minimum, do ONE logged exposure and the review. The streak that matters is the review, because the review regenerates the week.

## One honest flag

This protocol is built for the sub-clinical case: hesitation, initiation gap, post-event rumination, the stuff in your profile. If the anxiety is at a level where it's blocking classes, recruiting, or basic functioning, the evidence is unambiguous that guided beats unguided by a wide margin (g ≈ 0.83 vs 0.27). Purdue CAPS does CBT groups for exactly this, and using one alongside this plan isn't a fallback, it's choosing the stronger treatment arm. The plan works fine as a companion to that, and the strongest version of you would treat that decision the way you'd treat any other resourcing call: by the numbers.
