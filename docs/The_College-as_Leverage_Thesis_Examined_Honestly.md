# College-as-Leverage: Working Summary

## Core finding
College leverage is real but lumpy, unequal, and infrastructure-dependent. The defensible thesis: college creates compressed access to people, projects, mentors, and recruiting gates that are harder to reproduce later. The false thesis: prestige automatically confers invisible compounding advantages to anyone clever enough to "play it right." What you DO in college predicts more than the campus label, though much experience evidence is correlational, not fully causal. Bottom line: experiences > prestige, on average.

## The named evidence

Gallup-Purdue (the empirical center of gravity):
- Long-term work engagement and well-being tied more to experiences (mentorship, internships, sustained projects, extracurriculars) than to school type.
- Mentorship triple: graduates who had a professor who cared, one who excited them about learning, AND a mentor who encouraged their goals had >2x odds of work engagement. 57% with all three were engaged vs 25% without.
- Raposa et al. used the same dataset, nationally representative sample of 5,684 graduates, on faculty-student mentorship as critical to development.
- Higher undergrad debt linked to worse well-being, sharply lower thriving, and may inhibit entrepreneurship.
- Supportive relationships during college were more strongly tied to thriving than the experiential/deep-learning bundle alone. "Not where you go but how you do it."

Dale-Krueger (selectivity rebuttal):
- Comparing students accepted/rejected by similar schools, attending a more selective college did NOT on average raise earnings.
- BUT larger payoffs for students from disadvantaged backgrounds.

Heterogeneous subgroup effects (the real nuance):
- Hoekstra: sizable causal earnings premium for white men who barely got into a flagship state university.
- Ge, Isaac, Miller: selectivity raised earnings mainly for women, largely via longer labor-force attachment.
- Newer mobility work: low-SES students induced into elite colleges saw substantial positive earnings effects.
- Verdict: average effects modest, subgroup effects can be large. Leverage is stronger for lower-income, less-connected students and narrow-funnel fields; weaker for the already-networked.

UC extracurricular study (applied participation):
- Controlling for GPA, transfer/first-gen status, gender, ethnicity: club/org participants reported higher belonging and interpersonal skills, graduated on time more often.
- Seven years out, nonparticipants earned less than those who participated as leaders.
- First-gen, low-income, and transfer students had the HIGHEST non-participation rates: those who most need the surplus have least bandwidth to claim it.

## Real leverage points (use these)
1. Mentor access. Faculty/adult mentors give clarity, calibration, and confidence, not just introductions. Experimental youth-labor evidence shows mentors help via information and encouragement, not magical referrals.
2. Repeated peer contact. Sustained organizational participation, not symbolic titles.
3. Structured applied work. Multi-semester projects, internships, research, rotational programs. College lowers the transaction cost of trying things, a property of institutional design, not youth.
4. Recruiting windows. Narrow early-career funnels (e.g., Uber APM: small cohorts, rotations, product-leader mentorship) tied explicitly to the new-grad window.
5. Belonging and identity. Ages 18-25 are "emerging adulthood," plastic for identity formation. Good environments support exploration AND build belonging; belonging links to well-being and lower depression risk. The read is not "reinvent every semester."

## Overstated leverage points (discount these)
1. Alumni networks. Only 9% of graduates found theirs helpful/very helpful; 69% neutral, 22% unhelpful. Even top-50 schools only reach ~1 in 6. Networks are valuable only with structure, reciprocity norms, and a student who knows how to activate them.
2. Brand = outcome. Dale-Krueger again: premium largely disappears at similar admit sets. The Nature exceptional-achievement paper shows concentration in few institutions, but concentration is not causation. Prestige helps legibility, referrals, ambient ambition; it does not substitute for action.
3. "Cheapest time to fail." Breaks down under financial pressure: debt worsens well-being and thriving. The student working long hours or worrying about family finances is not in the same leverage landscape.

## Application to Purdue IBE
The platform is strong, more PM-adjacent than coastal discourse grants a public school:
- Curriculum ~44% business, 33.5% engineering; five semesters Innovation Lab; two years EPICS or VIP project work.
- IBE outcomes: avg full-time salary $77,494; 95% engaged at six months; 87% of Daniels grads had an internship; IBE interns avg $26/hr.
- PM club already built 13 events and 16 speaker collaborations with Google, PayPal, Microsoft, Salesloft.

The real gap vs Stanford/MIT is ecosystem density, not student capability. Elite schools offer more PM-specific infrastructure, activated alumni, ambient recruiter attention; MIT Sloan has a PM Lab, certificate, clubs, directories. There is NO clean public dataset on school-by-school APM/RPM acceptance rates. The delta is biggest at the ambiguous first screen (where pedigree and trusted brand proxies dominate) and narrows once you have shipped real work and earned one strong internship.

Operating model: manufacture the evidence elite students partly inherit from ecosystem.
- Use Innovation Lab / EPICS / VIP to ship products or decision systems with actual users.
- Convert that into an internship by junior year, even if titled SWE, data, product analyst, strategy/ops, or founder's office rather than straight PM.
- Use the PM club, speaker events, and alumni outreach to simulate network density.
- MIT Sloan PM guidance: companies want PM or closely related experience plus strong quant/technical skills; launched businesses count.

## When optimization backfires (warnings)
1. Extrinsic motivation is brittle. Intrinsic motivation tied to greater subjective well-being, meaning, and academic performance; extrinsic showed fewer positive relationships. Ambition organized purely around external validation is fragile.
2. Perfectionism harms. Academic stress, high perfectionism, rumination, and worry connect to compromised performance and to anxiety/depression. The "perfect resume, impoverished interior" student is a known pathology.
3. Sacrificing belonging costs you. Student loneliness 52% in 2025 (down from 58% in 2022); lonely students >4x as likely to face severe psychological distress. Social support and belonging tie to well-being, persistence, and lower depression risk. Optimizing away friendship and rest removes inputs to functioning itself.

## Synthesis
Treat college as guided compounding, not total optimization. The leverage is often partly made OF the life. Concrete operating model for a Purdue IBE sophomore:
- Build one compounding craft stack employers can verify.
- Make one applied arena your proving ground, ideally something that ships and has users.
- Cultivate one professor/adult mentor and one non-instrumental community you would care about without LinkedIn.
- Protect enough slack that exploration stays real, not theatrical.

The deepest correction: hindsight makes college look valuable because the compounding was real, but it hides how much came from ordinary things optimization culture downgrades: showing up repeatedly, joining something, asking for help, trying an internship that might not work, making friends, staying healthy enough to keep going. The leverage is real but less glamorous, less automatic, and more human than the discourse admits.
