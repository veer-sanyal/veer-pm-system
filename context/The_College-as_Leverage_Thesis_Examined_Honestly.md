# College-as-Leverage: Working Summary

## Core finding
College leverage is real but lumpy, unequal, and infrastructure-dependent. Defensible thesis: compressed access to people, projects, mentors, and recruiting gates harder to reproduce later. False thesis: prestige automatically confers invisible compounding advantages. What you DO predicts more than campus label (evidence correlational, not fully causal). Experiences > prestige, on average.

## Named Evidence

| Study | Key finding |
|---|---|
| **Gallup-Purdue** (n=5,684, nationally representative) | Engagement/well-being tied to experiences, not school type. Mentorship triple (caring professor + one who excited learning + goal-encouraging mentor): 57% engaged with all three vs. 25% without (>2x odds). Higher debt → worse well-being, lower thriving, may inhibit entrepreneurship. Supportive relationships tied more to thriving than experiential bundle alone. |
| **Raposa et al.** (same Gallup-Purdue dataset) | Faculty-student mentorship critical to development. |
| **Dale-Krueger** | More selective college did NOT on average raise earnings (comparing accepted/rejected by similar schools). Larger payoffs for disadvantaged-background students. |
| **Hoekstra** | Sizable causal earnings premium for white men who barely entered a flagship state university. |
| **Ge, Isaac, Miller** | Selectivity raised earnings mainly for women, via longer labor-force attachment. |
| **Mobility work** | Low-SES students induced into elite colleges saw substantial positive earnings effects. |
| **UC extracurricular study** | Club participants (controlling GPA, transfer/first-gen, gender, ethnicity): higher belonging, better interpersonal skills, on-time graduation. Seven years out, nonparticipants earned less than leader-participants. First-gen/low-income/transfer students had HIGHEST non-participation rates. |

Verdict: average selectivity effects modest; subgroup effects large. Leverage stronger for lower-income, less-connected students and narrow-funnel fields.

## Leverage Points

| Point | Use / Discount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mentor access | **Use** | Clarity, calibration, confidence—not just introductions. Help via information/encouragement, not magical referrals. |
| Repeated peer contact | **Use** | Sustained organizational participation, not symbolic titles. |
| Structured applied work | **Use** | Multi-semester projects, internships, research, rotational programs. College lowers transaction costs for trying things. |
| Recruiting windows | **Use** | Narrow funnels (e.g., Uber APM: small cohorts, rotations, product-leader mentorship) tied to the new-grad window. |
| Belonging (ages 18–25) | **Use** | "Emerging adulthood," plastic for identity. Good environments support exploration AND build belonging. Belonging links to well-being and lower depression risk. The read is not "reinvent every semester." |
| Alumni networks | **Discount** | 9% found helpful/very helpful; 69% neutral, 22% unhelpful. Top-50 schools reach ~1 in 6. Value requires structure, reciprocity norms, activation. |
| Brand = outcome | **Discount** | Dale-Krueger: premium disappears at similar admit sets. Nature exceptional-achievement paper shows concentration in few institutions, but concentration is not causation. Prestige aids legibility and ambient ambition; doesn't substitute for action. |
| "Cheapest time to fail" | **Discount** | Breaks down under financial pressure. Debt worsens well-being/thriving; high work-hours students are in a different landscape. |

## Application to Purdue IBE
Gap vs. Stanford/MIT is ecosystem density, not student capability.

**IBE platform:** ~44% business, 33.5% engineering; five semesters Innovation Lab; two years EPICS or VIP. Avg full-time salary $77,494; 95% engaged at six months; 87% of Daniels grads had an internship; IBE interns avg $26/hr. PM club: 13 events, 16 speaker collaborations (Google, PayPal, Microsoft, Salesloft).

Elite schools offer more PM-specific infrastructure, activated alumni, ambient recruiter attention; MIT Sloan has PM Lab, certificate, clubs, directories. No clean public dataset on school-by-school APM/RPM acceptance rates. Delta is biggest at the first screen (pedigree proxies) and narrows once you've shipped real work and one strong internship.

**Operating model:** manufacture the evidence elite students partly inherit.
- Innovation Lab/EPICS/VIP → ship products or decision systems with actual users.
- Convert into an internship by junior year—SWE, data, product analyst, strategy/ops, or founder's office counts.
- PM club + speaker events + alumni outreach → simulate network density.
- MIT Sloan PM guidance: companies want PM or closely related experience plus strong quant/technical skills; launched businesses count.

## Warnings
1. **Extrinsic motivation is brittle.** Intrinsic motivation → greater well-being, meaning, academic performance; extrinsic showed fewer positive relationships.
2. **Perfectionism harms.** High perfectionism, rumination, and academic stress connect to compromised performance and anxiety/depression.
3. **Sacrificing belonging costs you.** Student loneliness 52% in 2025 (down from 58% in 2022); lonely students >4x as likely to face severe psychological distress.

## Synthesis
Treat college as guided compounding, not total optimization. For a Purdue IBE sophomore:
- One compounding craft stack employers can verify.
- One applied arena that ships and has users.
- One professor/adult mentor; one non-instrumental community you'd care about without LinkedIn.
- Protect slack so exploration stays real, not theatrical.

Deepest correction: much leverage came from things optimization culture downgrades—showing up repeatedly, joining something, asking for help, trying an internship that might not work, making friends, staying healthy. The leverage is real but less glamorous, less automatic, and more human than the discourse admits.
