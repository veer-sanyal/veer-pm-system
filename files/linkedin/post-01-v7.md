## Post body

The biggest slice of India's digital-services exports isn't software anymore. I didn't expect that, so I traced it through the WTO's own numbers.

I spent the last three weeks building a public dashboard around one question: when a digital-trade rule shifts, like the WTO e-commerce moratorium, which of India's small businesses are most exposed? I built it for one reader in particular, a trade-policy analyst deciding which sectors are worth a closer look before a negotiation.

The category now on top is "other business services," which is mostly consulting, accounting, legal, and professional work sold across borders over the internet. It grew from under $7B in 2005 to about $92B, roughly 13x, while the rest of that category stayed flat. India's IT-export story is becoming a consulting story, and the usual headline figures keep it hidden inside a vague label.

What I care about more than the finding is how it's built. It's a v0, and the page says so. The exposure score adds two things, how many firms and how much digital trade, weighted equally, and I flagged that equal weighting as an open question rather than a result. The one link the public data can't actually support, tying a sector's trade to its individual small firms, is labeled on the page as a modeled assumption instead of buried. Every number is computed from the source data when the page builds, so nothing is typed by hand and nothing goes stale when the WTO updates.

Live walkthrough: https://veer-sanyal.github.io/india-msme-digital-trade-exposure/

If you build data products or work in policy: when you ship a v0 with known gaps, how do you decide what counts as useful now versus not ready yet?
