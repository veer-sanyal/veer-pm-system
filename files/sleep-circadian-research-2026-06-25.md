# Sleep-fix + circadian research (deep-research original, 2026-06-25)

Full evidence base for the sleep-schedule workstream. The operational protocol Veer
is actually running lives in `context/sleep-protocol.md` (read that for day-to-day);
this file is the deep-dive reference (the `files/` tier). Produced by the built-in
deep-research harness: 5 search angles, 22 sources fetched, 102 claims extracted, 25
verified by 3-vote adversarial check (24 confirmed, 1 killed).

Question: evidence-based, low-cost protocol for a 19-year-old to move from an irregular
11am-1pm wake to a sustained 9am wake before classes resume (~Aug 24). Cause is
primarily behavioral (screen-based revenge bedtime procrastination, not sleep-onset
insomnia) plus a likely ~2-4h circadian phase delay.

---

## Bottom line

The single most effective lever is a **rigidly fixed wake time held all 7 days,
regardless of when he fell asleep, plus no napping.** It rebuilds homeostatic sleep
pressure (Process S) so the late screen time collapses on its own. This beats trying
to force an earlier bedtime directly. Implementation intentions (if-then plans for the
specific screen obstacle) add real but modest value on top. Morning bright light and
low-dose afternoon melatonin are accelerants for the circadian piece, not the engine,
and the clock only moves ~30-50 min earlier per day, not the ~1h/day commonly assumed.

## Section 1 — Behavioral driver (primary, strongest evidence)

**Veer's pattern is textbook bedtime procrastination, a self-regulation / intention-action
gap, not a sleep-onset disorder.** [STRONG]
- Kroese et al. 2014 (Frontiers in Psychology, PMC4062817) define it as "failing to go
  to bed at the intended time, while no external circumstances prevent a person from
  doing so" — exactly his case. It correlates with low self-control (r=-0.52), with
  insufficient sleep (r=0.61), and shorter sleep (r=-0.49). Sleep-disordered participants
  were excluded, so the construct is explicitly distinct from inability to fall asleep.
- Kroese et al. 2016 (J Health Psychology, N=2431 representative Dutch sample): self-
  regulation's effect on insufficient sleep is *mediated* by bedtime procrastination,
  locating the lever in behavioral/motivational territory, not insomnia treatment.
- Caveat: these are cross-sectional. A state-resource-depletion debate (Kuhnel 2018) and
  2026 reframings (temporal discounting, unmet psychological needs) qualify the pure
  "trait self-control failure" story but do not move the lever to insomnia pharmacotherapy.

**Fixed wake time + no naps is the core lever, and it beats targeting bedtime directly.** [STRONG]
- Bootzin's Stimulus Control Therapy (UPenn CBT-I protocol), instruction #5: "Set your
  alarm and get up at the same time every morning irrespective of how much sleep you got
  during the night." Operationalized as a consistent wake time all 7 days with <1h
  difference between days off and workdays (this is also the weekend/social-drift control).
- The no-nap rule exists "to ensure that those with insomnia use the sleep deprivation
  from the prior night to facilitate falling asleep quickly on the next night" — i.e.
  preserving Process S so onset speeds up and the screen window self-corrects.
- Stimulus control is an effective *standalone* intervention vs control (Verreault et al.
  2024, J Sleep Research, network meta-analysis of 23 RCTs).
- Mechanism: fixing the wake time builds sleep drive, which is what makes an earlier
  bedtime actually possible. Merely instructing an earlier bedtime fails because the sleep
  pressure to support it is not there yet.
- Note: fixed wake is one of six SCT rules and the key lever, not literally the only rule.

**Implementation intentions / MCII add value beyond generic sleep hygiene.** [STRONG, modest effect]
- Valshtein, Oettingen & Gollwitzer 2020 (Psychology & Health 35(3):275-301; two
  preregistered RCTs, N=383 and N=221): if-then plans for personal obstacles to getting
  to bed on time reduced bedtime procrastination (MCII group slept ~33 min earlier vs ~14
  min control). In Study 2 MCII outperformed a sleep-hygiene control, via increased goal
  commitment.
- Caveats: undergraduate samples, short 1-3 week follow-up, ~19 min net effect, and a 2025
  daily-MCII registered report found weaker daily effects. Real but modest additive lever.

**Phone friction = remove the phone, not filter its light.** [behavioral lever STRONG; light-filtering MYTH]
- The high-confidence lever is physical separation from the device (stimulus control). The
  refuted multi-component claim (below) does not change this.
- Blue-light-blocking glasses: a 2025 systematic review/meta-analysis of double-blind RCTs
  (Frontiers in Neurology, PMC12668929) found no reliable actigraphic sleep benefit. MYTH.
- The strict "no screens 2h before bed" rule was not adjudicated with primary evidence
  here; treat as WEAK/unproven. The proven move is removing the phone, not dimming it.

**Refuted (killed 0-3):** the claim that multi-component (cognitive + behavioral +
emotional) intervention is *necessary* and purely behavioral levers are insufficient. A
single small pilot (n=32) showed a large effect for transdiagnostic CBT, but the broader
evidence keeps the behavioral lever (fixed wake + stimulus control) as a sufficient spine.

## Section 2 — Circadian support (adjunct, weaker guideline status)

**Morning bright light advances the clock only when timed AFTER the core body temperature
minimum (CBTmin); too early it DELAYS.** [STRONG mechanism]
- Khalsa et al. 2003 (J Physiol 549:945-952): ~5000-10,000 lux, 6.7h pulses. Delays when
  centered before CBTmin, advances when after, rapid crossover at CBTmin. Max single-pulse
  advance +2.01h vs delay -3.60h.
- In a phase-delayed person CBTmin sits late (near natural wake), so very-early light can
  land before CBTmin and delay. Timing must reference actual wake/CBTmin, not clock time.
  This is a direct argument for advancing gradually rather than cold-turkey.

**Low-dose 0.5mg melatonin as a clock-shifter beats dose-chasing; timing > dose.** [STRONG]
- Burgess et al. 2010 (J Clin Endocrinol Metab 95(7):3325-31, PMID 20410229): "Maximum
  advances occurred when 0.5 mg melatonin was taken in the afternoon, 2-4 h before the
  DLMO, or 9-11 h before sleep midpoint." When each dose is given at its optimal time, 0.5
  and 3.0 mg yield similarly sized shifts (P>0.60).
- AASM/Auger 2015: "timing is more important than dose"; higher doses add soporific
  effects. This distinguishes the low pre-DLMO clock-shifting dose from a high sedating
  lights-out dose.
- Crowley & Eastman protocol (PMC4344919): 0.5mg ~5h before bedtime, chosen to hit the
  advance portion of the 0.5mg PRC and avoid evening sleepiness.

**Combined light + melatonin is most effective but only ~0.5-0.8h/day.** [STRONG]
- Crowley & Eastman 2015 (Sleep Medicine, PMC4344919; 50 healthy young adults): morning
  light ~3500-6880 lux (mean 4829) within 5 min of waking + 0.5mg afternoon melatonin +
  1h/day advanced sleep. Best (2h-light) group advanced 2.4±0.8h over 3 days = ~0.8h/day;
  weaker light ~0.6h/day. The combination is the largest advance (additive, not synergistic).
- Crucially: although the imposed *schedule* shifted 1h/day, measured circadian phase
  advanced only ~0.8h/day max. The clock lags the schedule. Calibrate to ~30-50 min/day.
- A two-morning adolescent study (~6000 lux, 2.5h exposure) advanced DLMO ~1.0h over a
  weekend vs ~0.6h with shorter/room light.

**For adult DSWPD, morning light as MONOTHERAPY got NO recommendation; melatonin is WEAK-FOR.** [guideline-backed adjudication]
- AASM/Auger et al. 2015 (J Clin Sleep Med 11(10):1199): "insufficient evidence to support
  efficacy of postawakening light therapy (monotherapy)... No recommendation," downgraded
  from a prior guideline rating. Positive light support exists only for children/adolescents
  and only *in conjunction with* behavioral treatment (WEAK FOR, LOW quality). Strategically
  timed melatonin: positive but WEAK FOR, second-tier confidence.
- Net: "morning sun alone fixes a delayed schedule" is weaker than popularly stated for
  adults. The evidence elevates the light + behavioral combination. This is why fixed wake
  + stimulus control is the spine and light/melatonin are adjuncts.

## Section 3 — Distinguishing the two drivers, and sequencing

**Distinguishing test:** if he falls asleep fast (sleep-onset latency <~20 min) once the
phone is down, the limiter is behavioral procrastination (he could sleep but does not
disengage). If he lies awake a long time after lights-out despite wanting to sleep, a
circadian phase delay (or true onset difficulty) is contributing. (Clinically standard
inference; the SLEEP 2025 abstract found later DLMO correlates with higher bedtime
procrastination, so both can co-occur.)

**Sequencing:** install the behavioral spine FIRST (fixed wake + no naps + phone friction
+ if-then plans), because rising homeostatic pressure both shortens onset and is the
precondition that lets earlier bedtimes stick, and because AASM endorses light only WITH
behavioral treatment. Add morning light (referenced to actual wake) and afternoon 0.5mg
melatonin as accelerants for the circadian component, calibrated to ~0.5h/day.

**Bad-night rule:** keep the FIXED wake time anyway. Do not sleep in to recover, since that
bleeds off the very sleep pressure that fixes the next night.

## Section 5 — Leading indicators vs noise

Worth tracking (these predict the schedule moving earlier):
1. **Wake-time consistency** (actual vs target). DSWPD patients show significantly more
   variable wake times and total sleep time than controls (Murray et al., Sleep Medicine,
   PMC5514558); variability is the signal.
2. **Sleep-onset latency** once the phone is down (the homeostatic-pressure readout; should
   shorten as Process S rebuilds).
3. **Time of first phone-down** (the behavioral-lever readout).

Noise (do not track): total sleep hours night-to-night, subjective "sleep quality" scores,
wearable sleep-stage breakdowns. These are noisy, demotivating, and do not predict the shift.

## Section 6 — System-design adjudication (wake-anchored rebuild vs hard fixed anchor)

The evidence favors a **hard fixed wake-time anchor** as the accountability target rather
than rebuilding each day's plan from the actual (drifting) wake time, because the fixed
rise time *is* the active ingredient (Bootzin SCT instruction #5; <1h all-7-day rule;
no-nap homeostatic rationale). A daily wake-anchored rebuild from an 11am-1pm wake removes
exactly that pressure: it accommodates drift instead of correcting it.

The defensible role for a wake-signal mechanism (Veer's `initialize` idea) is as a
**transition-phase tool**: during the ~1-2 week advance, the body genuinely cannot jump to
9am (~0.5h/day circadian rate), so an incrementally-advancing fixed target is evidence-
consistent. Once at 9am, it becomes a hard fixed anchor and the signal drops to logging
and graceful-degradation only. Confidence: MEDIUM. No head-to-head RCT compares wake-
anchored-rebuild vs fixed-anchor planning systems; this extrapolates the SCT mechanism.

This aligns with the existing system's no-streaks / recoverable-consistency design (the
<1h-drift tolerance is exactly "consistency, not perfection") and its implementation-
intention + WOOP machinery (point it at the bedtime obstacle).

## Caveats (scope limits, stated honestly)

1. Bedtime-procrastination correlations (Kroese) are cross-sectional: behavioral construct
   and hypothesized lever, not proven causation.
2. Circadian phase-advance figures come from controlled-lab / healthy-young-adult samples,
   not free-living delayed teens. Real-world advance is likely slower; ~0.5h/day is the
   prudent planning assumption (the ~0.8h/day is a lab ceiling).
3. AASM/Auger 2015 ratings are WEAK-FOR / NO-RECOMMENDATION on LOW-quality evidence for the
   circadian adjuncts; the guideline warns recommendation strength is not a 1:1 map to
   evidence quality.
4. MCII RCTs use undergraduates, short follow-up, modest effects.
5. Myths flagged but not primary-tested here: blue-light glasses (separately refuted) and
   the strict "no screens 2h before bed" rule. Treat as weak/unproven; the proven lever is
   removing the phone.
6. The section-6 system recommendation is an extrapolation from the SCT mechanism (medium
   confidence).
7. CBTmin/DLMO are not measured; light/melatonin timing is estimated from habitual
   wake/bed and adjusted empirically. Melatonin is a supplement; 0.5mg is the clock-shifting
   target but product accuracy varies.
