# The College-as-Leverage Thesis Examined Honestly

The honest version of the thesis is not that college is a magical four-year portal and not that “where you go” is destiny. It is that college can create unusually compressed access to people, projects, mentors, and recruiting gates that are harder to reproduce later. The best evidence supports that weaker, more conditional claim. It does **not** support the stronger tech-discourse version in which college automatically confers invisible compounding advantages to anyone clever enough to “play it right.” In the strongest causal and quasi-causal work, institutional selectivity often shrinks dramatically once you compare students who were admitted to similar schools; in the best experience-level evidence, what students *do* in college often predicts more than the campus label, but many of those experience findings are correlational rather than fully causal. citeturn24view3turn29search1turn28search6turn25view5

A fair summary is this: the leverage is real, but it is **lumpy, unequal, and infrastructure-dependent**. It is strongest where colleges provide repeated contact with motivated peers, unusually accessible mentors, structured experimentation through internships and multi-semester projects, and access to narrow recruiting windows for early-career roles. It is overstated when it is treated as a general law of youth, as if every ambitious student is automatically embedded in a Stanford-like network. It is also overstated when brand is mistaken for substance, or when students are told to optimize relentlessly without regard to belonging, health, and the basic fact that the social substrate of college is itself one of the things that creates later leverage. citeturn23search0turn22search11turn27view1turn27view6turn21search3turn21search10

## What survives honest scrutiny

The closest thing to a hard empirical center of gravity is this: **college experiences matter a lot, and school prestige matters less on average than enthusiasts usually imply**. The Gallup-Purdue work found that long-term outcomes such as work engagement and well-being were more strongly related to experiences like mentorship, internships, sustained projects, and extracurricular involvement than to school type, and it also found that very few graduates had the full bundle of those experiences. In other words, there is a real “use college well” effect in the data, but it is about access and participation, not just admissions status. citeturn23search0turn24view3turn30view0

At the same time, the strongest selection-aware selectivity studies do not bless the grandiose version of the thesis. Dale and Krueger’s classic approach compared students who were accepted and rejected by similar schools and found that, on average, attending a more selective college did **not** raise earnings relative to comparable students who attended a less selective option; they did, however, find larger payoffs for students from more disadvantaged backgrounds. Other work complicates the picture rather than overturning it: Hoekstra found a sizable causal earnings premium for white men who barely got into a flagship state university, while later work by Ge, Isaac, and Miller found that more-selective attendance raised earnings mainly for women, largely through longer labor-force attachment. Newer mobility work also suggests that low-SES students induced to attend elite colleges can experience substantial and positive earnings effects. The right conclusion is not “selectivity does nothing” or “selectivity decides everything,” but “average effects are modest and subgroup effects can be large.” citeturn29search1turn29search16turn29search21turn28search6turn28search18

That matters because tech discourse frequently treats hindsight leverage as if it were universal. The evidence instead points to **heterogeneity**: leverage is stronger for students who are lower-income, less connected, or in fields with narrow recruiting funnels; weaker for already well-networked students; and often contingent on whether the school actually organizes the relevant ecosystem. That is why a good state-school program with strong experiential structure can be more “leveraged” for a given student than a more prestigious campus used passively. citeturn29search1turn28search18turn24view5turn25view5

## Where college leverage is real

The most defensible leverage points are **mentor access, repeated peer contact, and structured applied work**. Gallup-Purdue reported that graduates who recalled a professor who cared about them, one who made them excited about learning, and a mentor who encouraged their goals had more than double the odds of being engaged at work; 57% of those with all three were engaged at work versus 25% without that support. The same dataset also underlies Raposa and coauthors’ paper on faculty-student mentorship, which used a nationally representative sample of 5,684 graduates and argued that supportive mentor relationships are critical to development and academic success. Adjacent experimental evidence on mentoring in youth labor markets also suggests that mentors help mainly through information and encouragement rather than magical referrals, which is important: what faculty access often gives is *clarity, calibration, and confidence*, not just introductions. citeturn24view3turn30view0turn24view2turn23search21turn23search6

The next real leverage point is **applied participation**, especially when it is sustained and not purely symbolic. The University of California study on extracurricular participation is unusually useful because it goes beyond vibes: after accounting for differences in GPA, transfer status, first-generation status, gender, and ethnicity, students who participated in organizations and clubs reported higher belonging and interpersonal skills and were more likely to graduate on time; in one model, club participation was associated with higher odds of timely graduation, and the study’s earnings model found that seven years after graduation, nonparticipants earned less than those who had participated as leaders, controlling for race, discipline, and GPA. That does not prove every club title matters; it does suggest that repeated organizational participation can create real developmental and labor-market value. citeturn25view5turn26view0turn30view5

The “low cost of failure” claim is also partly real, but only in a specific institutional sense. It is less that twenty-year-olds possess mystical entrepreneurial courage than that college packs experimentation into lower-stakes containers: a semester project, a summer internship, a student club, a founder on campus, a research collaboration, an early-career rotational program. Stanford MS&E’s own undergraduate career page frames internships as exploration and says, in effect, that the worst case is a bad fit for 8–12 weeks and some learning; Uber’s APM page shows how some of the most coveted product pipelines are explicitly tied to the “new college graduate” window and structured around rotations and mentorship. So yes, college often lowers the *transaction costs* of trying things. But that is a property of institutional design, not a property of youth alone. citeturn27view0turn27view2turn24view5turn24view6

There is also a genuine **identity and belonging** dimension that tech discourse often notices but misdescribes. Developmental psychology treats ages roughly 18–25 as “emerging adulthood,” a period marked by identity exploration, instability, self-focus, and a sense of possibility; other work describes identity formation as a core developmental task across adolescence and emerging adulthood. That makes college unusually plastic in a human sense: people are still deciding what they value, whom they admire, and what work feels like “theirs.” But the useful reading here is not “reinvent yourself every semester.” It is that good college environments support exploration while also building belonging, and belonging is linked to well-being, engagement, and lower depression risk. citeturn18search11turn18search18turn18search21turn21search3turn21search19turn21search10

## Where the thesis is overstated

The most overstated leverage point is probably the **alumni network**. Gallup’s alumni survey found that only 9% of graduates said their undergraduate alumni network had been helpful or very helpful in the job market; 69% said it was neither helpful nor unhelpful, and 22% said it was unhelpful. Even among alumni from top-50 U.S. News schools, the figure rose only to about one in six. That is a devastating corrective to the romantic idea that college hands you a permanent job-market exoskeleton. Alumni networks are not inert assets sitting in a vault. They are only valuable when there is real structure, norms of reciprocity, and a student who knows how to activate them. citeturn22search11turn22search22

The next overstatement is the **brand-equals-outcome** story. Dale and Krueger remain the classic rebuttal: once you compare students with similar admit sets, the average earnings premium from selectivity largely disappears. Even where elite education does show large apparent advantages, some of that is descriptive concentration rather than clean causation. A recent Nature paper found that exceptional achievement in the United States is strongly associated with a very small set of institutions; that is important, but it demonstrates concentration, not that attending one of those schools mechanically causes exceptional outcomes for any given student. Prestige helps with legibility, referrals, and ambient ambition density. It does not reliably substitute for action. citeturn29search1turn29search16turn28search21

The “college is the cheapest time to fail” line also breaks down once money enters the picture. Gallup-Purdue found that higher undergraduate debt was associated with worse well-being, that thriving fell sharply as debt rose, and that higher debt may inhibit entrepreneurship and business formation. For students with meaningful financial pressure, the college years may not feel like playful slack at all; they may feel like constrained triage. The student who works long hours, transfers, commutes, or worries about family finances is not living inside the same leverage landscape as the student whose only job is self-optimization. citeturn24view3turn30view1turn25view5

That last point matters because access to the purported leverage is unequal **inside the same university**. The University of California study found that first-generation, low-income, and transfer students had the highest rates of non-participation in clubs and organizations. In plain English: the students who could most benefit from the social and informational surplus of college are often the students with the least spare bandwidth to claim it. Any thesis that ignores this becomes less a theory of leverage than a theory of privilege. citeturn25view5turn26view0

## Purdue IBE and the actual PM-recruiting delta

For a sophomore in Purdue’s IBE program, the honest read is that the school contains **real leverage**, and more of it than coastal-tech discourse usually grants to a public university. The official IBE report describes a curriculum that is roughly 44% business and 33.5% engineering, with five semesters of Innovation Lab and two full years of EPICS or VIP project work. The Daniels School’s undergraduate outcomes report shows IBE graduates with an average full-time salary of $77,494 and 95% reporting engagement at six months; the same school reports that 87% of graduates had at least one internship, with IBE students averaging a $26 hourly internship wage. This is not a weak platform. It is a strong, unusually PM-adjacent state-school platform. citeturn24view5turn24view6turn30view2

The key difference from Stanford or MIT is not raw student capability. It is **ecosystem density**. Stanford’s MS&E undergraduate careers page explicitly normalizes exploration and shows undergraduates interning across industries, which is useful because it punctures a myth: even at Stanford, there is not a single frictionless conveyor belt into PM. But Stanford still sits inside a much denser ambient network of startups, product people, and Bay Area employers. MIT’s publicly visible PM ecosystem is especially strong on the Sloan side: clubs, jobs, events, industry advisors, alumni directories, a PM Lab, and a PM Certificate. That is evidence of institutionally organized PM density, though it is more grad-school-heavy than a pure undergraduate comparison would ideally be. Uber’s APM program, meanwhile, makes clear that these roles are real but narrow: they are explicit new-grad funnels with small cohorts, rotations, and product-leader mentorship. citeturn27view0turn27view1turn27view2

So what is the actual delta for a state-school candidate versus a Stanford/MIT candidate for top PM roles? The honest answer is that **there is no clean public dataset showing school-by-school acceptance rates into APM/RPM/elite PM cohorts**. What the official evidence does show is that elite schools often offer more PM-specific infrastructure, more activated alumni, and more ambient recruiter attention, while Purdue offers strong substance but less ambient serendipity. That makes the gap feel larger in college than it often is later. The best inference from the available evidence is that the delta is biggest at the ambiguous first screen—where pedigree, referrals, and trusted brand proxies matter most—and narrows once a candidate has shipped real work, built technical credibility, and earned one strong internship. citeturn27view1turn27view2turn27view6turn28search21

What does that mean in practice for Purdue IBE? It means the transferable leverage is not the coastal-Twitter fantasy that “you’re one dorm conversation away from a unicorn.” The transferable leverage is more concrete: multi-semester product-like projects, a hybrid business/engineering narrative, an internship-rich school, and a PM club that has already built 13 events and 16 speaker collaborations with leaders from Google, PayPal, Microsoft, and Salesloft. The pathway is less ambient, more self-authored. MIT Sloan’s own PM guidance says companies want PM experience or closely related experience plus strong quantitative and technical skills, and that candidates who have launched businesses may also have developed PM capabilities. Purdue IBE gives you a credible base for exactly those proxy signals if you use it deliberately. citeturn27view6turn27view1turn24view5turn24view6

The practical way the gap gets closed is not mystical. It is by turning Purdue’s structure into legible PM evidence: use Innovation Lab, EPICS, or VIP to ship products or decision systems with actual users; convert that into an internship by junior year, even if the title is SWE intern, data intern, product analyst, strategy and ops, or founder’s office rather than straight PM; and then use the PM club, speaker events, and alumni outreach to simulate some of the network density elite students get by default. In other words, the state-school strategy is to **manufacture the evidence that elite-school students partly inherit from ecosystem**. citeturn27view1turn27view2turn24view5turn24view6turn27view6

## When optimization backfires

The failure mode of treating college as pure leverage is not hypothetical. The psychology literature is pretty clear that **extrinsic and controlled forms of motivation are weaker foundations for well-being than intrinsic or autonomous motivation**. A study of first-year university students found that intrinsic motivation was associated with greater subjective well-being, meaning in life, and academic performance, while extrinsic motivations showed fewer positive relationships. A large self-determination-theory meta-analysis likewise shows that different motivational styles matter for student outcomes. That does not mean ambition is bad; it means ambition organized entirely around external validation is brittle. citeturn20search1turn20search32turn20search12

The mental-health literature strengthens that warning. Studies of college students link academic stress to poor mental well-being, show that high levels of perfectionism can compromise both performance and psychological well-being, and connect perfectionism, academic stress, rumination, and worry to anxiety and depressive symptoms. These are exactly the pathologies you would expect to see in the “perfectly optimized résumé, impoverished interior life” student. The point is not that planning is harmful; it is that optimization can become a self-undermining operating system when every class, activity, friendship, and summer is evaluated only for signal value. citeturn19search5turn19search3turn19search28

The social evidence is even more direct. Healthy Minds reports that high loneliness among students, while improved from 58% in 2022, was still 52% in 2025. Trellis found that more than half of college students in the 2024 Student Financial Wellness Survey felt lonely sometimes or always. Active Minds reported that college students who feel lonely are more than four times as likely to experience severe psychological distress. Separate studies tie social support and belonging to better mental health and well-being, and even to lower depression risk. So the student who optimizes away friendship, community, rest, and non-instrumental joy may be sacrificing not just “college memories,” but inputs that are themselves strongly related to functioning and persistence. citeturn21search20turn21search9turn21search21turn21search10turn21search3turn21search19

There is also a subtler regret case: the belief that every hour not spent compounding is wasted. Gallup-Purdue’s own results suggest otherwise. Supportive relationships during college were more strongly associated with thriving in all areas of well-being than the bundle of experiential/deep-learning items alone. Graduates who felt supported were far more likely to thrive than those who did not, and Gallup’s broader conclusion was that it is not simply “where you go” but “how you do it.” Read carefully, that is not an endorsement of relentless optimization. It is an endorsement of serious work **embedded in human relationships**. citeturn24view3turn30view1

## A workable synthesis

The most defensible synthesis is that college should be treated as **a period of guided compounding rather than total optimization**. The evidence says you should care about mentorship, applied work, organizational participation, recruiting windows, and the structural opportunities that exist only while you are a student. It also says you should care about belonging, intrinsic motivation, and social support because they are not “soft extras”; they are tied to well-being, persistence, and later functioning. The false choice is between “use the leverage” and “actually live.” In the data, the leverage is often partly made *of* the life. citeturn24view3turn20search1turn21search3turn21search10

For a Purdue IBE sophomore, the honest operating model is simple. Build one compounding craft stack that employers can verify. Make one applied arena your proving ground, ideally something that ships and has users. Intentionally cultivate one professor or adult mentor and one non-instrumental community that you would still care about if LinkedIn did not exist. And protect enough slack that exploration remains possible rather than theatrical. That is how you use college leverage without becoming colonized by it. The student who graduates with strong skills, one credible internship, a few real allies, and a life that still feels inhabited has probably used college better than the student who extracted every visible signal and left with no inner center. citeturn24view5turn24view6turn27view6turn24view2turn24view3turn21search3

The deepest correction to the college-as-leverage thesis, then, is this: hindsight often makes college look valuable because the compounding was real, but hindsight also hides how much of that compounding came from ordinary things that optimization culture downgrades—showing up repeatedly, joining something, asking a professor for help, trying an internship that might not work, making friends, staying healthy enough to keep going. The leverage is not imaginary. It is just much less glamorous, much less automatic, and much more human than the discourse usually admits. citeturn24view3turn25view5turn27view0turn22search11turn21search10
